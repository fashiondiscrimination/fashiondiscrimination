Cover retrospective

In 2020, a New York Times investigation [1] reveals the "toxic" and "racist" environment at US Vogue. Anna Wintour, editor-in-chief "fostered a work environment that segregated women of color." The magazine's aesthetic reinforces an ideal of white, Euro-centric beauty. Sometimes we can ask ourselves if Vogue exists in the media more for its scandals than for its fashion articles.

50 shades of Rihanna

50 shades of Rihanna

Do you think somebody forgot to tell Vogue's photo editor that Rihanna is black?

Vogue has been in the industry for over a century but they have only recently started to incorporate darker models. Before 2012 models such as Rihanna and Halle Berry were on the cover of Vogue but are both light skin.

On Rihanna's five Vogue covers, her skin tone lightness spanned 40% of the lightness range that we found among cover models. The creator of fenty beauty was a victim of whitewashing, like many black people in the fashion industry. ‘Whitewashing’ in the fashion industry refers to the digital modifying or altering of the skin of a non-white person to make it appear “whiter”. It's usually employed to make people of colour seem to conform more to what are thought to be “Eurocentric beauty standards”

Racism is Not En Vogue

Racism is Not En Vogue

Over the years, Gisele's skin color has evolved. In the early 2000s, we can see a very pale complexion, especially in 2004. While in 2018, she is darker. Beauty standards have evolved over the years.

In 2007, Vogue announced its cover starring LeBron James and Gisele Bundchen, James was the first black man to grace its cover. On the cover, we can see Gisele Bündchen next to the basketball player Lebron James leaping and roaring, grabbing him by the waist. An image that referred directly to the first posters of King Kong.

Tamara Walker said : “If more people of color worked for Vogue in positions of editorial authority, perhaps someone in the room might have been able to read the image the way so many of us are reading it now, and had the power to do something about it”

Tyler Mitchell: the photographer who made history with Queen B

After 125 years of the magazine's existence, 2018 is a historic year for Vogue. Tyler Mitchell is the first black phorographer to shoot a Vogue US cover.

Beyoncé went on to address the power structures that prevent true representation and explained why advocating for diversity in her creative projects matters. "If people in powerful positions continue to hire and cast only people who look like them, sound like them, come from the same neighborhoods they grew up in, they will never have a greater understanding of experiences different from their own," she explained. "They will hire the same models, curate the same art, cast the same actors over and over again, and we will all lose. The beauty of social media is it’s completely democratic. Everyone has a say. Everyone’s voice counts, and everyone has a chance to paint the world from their own perspective."

That’s progress, but it doesn’t change the fact that Lupita is the only dark-skinned professional model on the cover of Vogue in the past nineteen years.

Black Models Matter

×
Black Models Matter

In 2012 Serena Williams was the first black dark skin model to be on the cover of Vogue; following her was Lupita Nyong’o and Michelle Obama. Lupita Nyong’o is been on the cover of Vogue 4 times,she's the darkest of them all.The majority of black women on the cover of vogue are light skin. Inclusion does not equal tokenism.

Tokenism is the practice of only including a few people of a minority, using that small group to represent every single person of that minority. This is seen everywhere in fashion, once they find one black dark skin model they keep them and don’t search for more.

In an interview with the Times of London, Chanel Iman said ‘Designers have told me, ‘We already found one black girl. We don’t need you any more’'.

T-Age Anadi, 26, a Black model signed to Wilhelmina, said models of color, on top of having a harder time getting jobs in high fashion, have to deal with makeup artists, hair stylists and photographers that don't know how to work with their coloring or hair texture. "To be inclusive is not just to have a Black model, but also a makeup artist that doesn't make you look ashy, or a hair stylist that knows how to deal with thick, curly hair, or a photographer that knows what lighting looks good on your skin," she said.

Society is becoming more aware of the issues that dark skin faces and there is a slow tilt in representation, hopefully Vogue and many other fashion magazines will evolve.

Anna Wintour apologises for not giving space to black people at Vogue : “Vogue has not found enough ways to elevate and give space to black editors, writers, photographers, designers and other creators.”. If she understood the systemic system, and cared about it, the editor for three decades would have changed things.
